Topic 1: Order of Operations (BIDMAS)

BIDMAS helps you remember the correct order to perform calculations:

  • Brackets
  • Indices (Powers and Roots)
  • Division
  • Multiplication
  • Addition
  • Subtraction

(Division & Multiplication are done from left to right, and so are Addition & Subtraction.)

Example: 2 + 3 × 4

Solution: 2 + (3 × 4) = 2 + 12 = 14

Example: (2 + 3) × 4

Solution: (5) × 4 = 20

Topic 2: Number Properties

Prime Numbers

A prime number is a whole number greater than 1 that has exactly two factors: 1 and itself. (e.g., 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13...)

Prime Factorisation

Writing a number as a product of its prime factors. A factor tree can be used.

Example: Write 81 as a product of its prime factors.

Using a factor tree: 81 → 3 × 27 → 3 × 3 × 9 → 3 × 3 × 3 × 3

81 = 3 × 3 × 3 × 3 = 34

Example: Write 68 as a product of its prime factors.

Using a factor tree: 68 → 2 × 34 → 2 × 2 × 17

68 = 2 × 2 × 17 = 22 × 17

Multiples and Lowest Common Multiple (LCM)

Multiples of a number are found by multiplying that number by integers (1, 2, 3, ...).

The Lowest Common Multiple (LCM) of two or more numbers is the smallest number that is a multiple of all of them.

Example: Find the LCM of 15 and 20.

Multiples of 15: 15, 30, 45, 60, 75, ...

Multiples of 20: 20, 40, 60, 80, ...

The LCM of 15 and 20 is 60.

Topic 3: Algebra

Simplifying Expressions (Collecting Like Terms)

Combine terms that have the same variable part (and the same power).

Example: Simplify 5x + 3x + 2y + 4y

(5x + 3x) + (2y + 4y) = 8x + 6y

Expanding Brackets

Single Bracket: Multiply the term outside the bracket by each term inside the bracket.

a(b + c) = ab + ac

Example: Expand 3(x + 6)

3 × x + 3 × 6 = 3x + 18

Double Brackets (FOIL method): Multiply each term in the first bracket by each term in the second bracket.

(a + b)(c + d) = ac + ad + bc + bd

Example: Expand and simplify (x + 3)(x + 5)

x(x + 5) + 3(x + 5) = x2 + 5x + 3x + 15 = x2 + 8x + 15

Factorising

Common Factors: Find the Highest Common Factor (HCF) of the terms and take it outside the bracket.

Example: Factorise 10x + 15

HCF of 10x and 15 is 5.

10x + 15 = 5(2x + 3)

Quadratics of the form x2 + bx + c: Find two numbers that multiply to make 'c' and add to make 'b'.

Example: Factorise x2 + 10x + 16

Need two numbers that multiply to 16 and add to 10. These are 2 and 8.

x2 + 10x + 16 = (x + 2)(x + 8)

Laws of Indices

  • Multiplication: am × an = am+n
  • Division: am ÷ an = am-n (or am/an = am-n)
  • Power of a Power: (am)n = amn
  • Zero Index: a0 = 1 (for a ≠ 0)
  • One Index: a1 = a

Example: Simplify (52)6 leaving your answer in index form.

(52)6 = 52×6 = 512

Example: Simplify 5x4y5 ÷ x3y3

5x4-3y5-3 = 5x1y2 = 5xy2

Solving Quadratic Equations by Factorising

1. Rearrange the equation to equal zero (ax2 + bx + c = 0).

2. Factorise the quadratic expression.

3. Set each factor equal to zero and solve for x.

Example: Solve x2 + 5x + 6 = 0

1. Already equals zero.

2. Factorise: (x + 2)(x + 3) = 0

3. Set factors to zero:

x + 2 = 0 => x = -2

x + 3 = 0 => x = -3

Solutions: x = -2 or x = -3

Topic 4: Sequences

Finding Terms of a Sequence

Substitute the term number (n=1 for 1st term, n=2 for 2nd term, etc.) into the nth term rule.

Example: The nth term of a sequence is 3n + 2.

a) First three terms:

n=1: 3(1) + 2 = 5

n=2: 3(2) + 2 = 8

n=3: 3(3) + 2 = 11

b) Tenth term:

n=10: 3(10) + 2 = 32

Finding the nth Term of an Arithmetic Sequence

An arithmetic sequence has a common difference (d) between terms.

The nth term rule is often in the form: an + b or dn + (zeroth term).

1. Find the common difference (d). This is the 'a' or 'd' in 'an' or 'dn'.

2. Find the 'zeroth term' (the term before the first term) or use the formula: d × n + (first term - d).

Example: Find the nth term of 2, 5, 8, 11, 14...

1. Common difference (d) = 5 - 2 = 3.

2. The sequence increases by 3 each time (like the 3 times table). 3n would be: 3, 6, 9, 12, 15... To get from 3n to our sequence (2, 5, 8, 11, 14), we subtract 1. So, nth term = 3n - 1.

Alternatively, using first term - d: 3n + (2 - 3) = 3n - 1.

Topic 5: Ratio and Proportion

Sharing in a Ratio

1. Add the parts of the ratio to find the total number of parts.

2. Divide the total amount by the total number of parts to find the value of one part.

3. Multiply the value of one part by each number in the ratio.

Example: Two people share £70 in the ratio 3:2.

1. Total parts = 3 + 2 = 5 parts.

2. Value of one part = £70 ÷ 5 = £14.

3. Person 1 gets: 3 × £14 = £42.

Person 2 gets: 2 × £14 = £28.

(Check: £42 + £28 = £70)

Topic 6: Percentages of Amounts

To find a percentage of an amount (non-calculator methods):

  • Find 10%: Divide the amount by 10.
  • Find 1%: Divide the amount by 100 (or divide 10% by 10).
  • Find 5%: Halve 10%.
  • Find 50%: Halve the amount.
  • Build up to the required percentage using these parts.

Example: Calculate 20% of £170.

10% of £170 = £170 ÷ 10 = £17.

20% of £170 = 2 × 10% = 2 × £17 = £34.

Example: Calculate 15% of 60 kg.

10% of 60 kg = 6 kg.

5% of 60 kg = (10% of 60 kg) ÷ 2 = 6 kg ÷ 2 = 3 kg.

15% of 60 kg = 10% + 5% = 6 kg + 3 kg = 9 kg.

Topic 7: Probability

Probability of an event = (Number of favourable outcomes) / (Total number of possible outcomes).

Probability is always between 0 (impossible) and 1 (certain).

P(event not happening) = 1 - P(event happening).

Tree Diagrams

Used for two or more events happening in sequence.

  • Multiply probabilities ALONG the branches to find the probability of a sequence of outcomes (AND rule).
  • Probabilities on branches from a single point must add up to 1.
  • Add probabilities DOWN the list of final outcomes for different ways to achieve a result (OR rule for mutually exclusive events).

Example: The probability of a biased coin landing on heads is 2/5. Complete the tree diagram for two flips.

P(Head) = 2/5, so P(Tail) = 1 - 2/5 = 3/5.

Tree Diagram Branches:

  • Flip 1 (H) → Flip 2 (H): P(H and H) = (2/5) × (2/5) = 4/25
  • Flip 1 (H) → Flip 2 (T): P(H and T) = (2/5) × (3/5) = 6/25
  • Flip 1 (T) → Flip 2 (H): P(T and H) = (3/5) × (2/5) = 6/25
  • Flip 1 (T) → Flip 2 (T): P(T and T) = (3/5) × (3/5) = 9/25
(Sum of probabilities = 4/25 + 6/25 + 6/25 + 9/25 = 25/25 = 1)

Venn Diagrams

Used to show relationships between sets.

  • The rectangle (ε) represents the universal set (all possible outcomes).
  • Circles represent specific events or sets.
  • The overlapping section (intersection) represents outcomes in BOTH A AND B.
  • The total area covered by circles represents outcomes in A OR B (or both).

Example (Venn Diagram Setup): In a group of 20 students, 12 enjoy sport (S), 10 enjoy music (M), and 4 enjoy neither.

1. Students enjoying at least one activity = Total - Neither = 20 - 4 = 16.

2. Number in (S or M) = Number in S + Number in M - Number in (S and M).

16 = 12 + 10 - Number in (S and M)

16 = 22 - Number in (S and M)

Number in (S and M) = 22 - 16 = 6 (this is the intersection).

3. Only S = Total S - (S and M) = 12 - 6 = 6.

4. Only M = Total M - (S and M) = 10 - 6 = 4.

Check: Only S (6) + Only M (4) + Both (6) + Neither (4) = 20. Correct.

Conditional Probability (Dependent Events)

When the outcome of one event affects the outcome of another. Often involves "without replacement".

Example: A bag contains 5 red and 4 blue balls. Two balls are drawn without replacement. Find P(2 red balls).

Total balls initially = 5 + 4 = 9.

P(1st is Red) = 5/9.

After taking one red ball, there are 4 red balls left and 8 total balls.

P(2nd is Red | 1st was Red) = 4/8.

P(Red and Red) = P(1st Red) × P(2nd Red | 1st Red) = (5/9) × (4/8) = 20/72 (which simplifies to 5/18).

Topic 8: Operations with Negative Numbers

Rules for multiplication and division:

  • Positive × Positive = Positive
  • Positive × Negative = Negative
  • Negative × Positive = Negative
  • Negative × Negative = Positive

(Same rules apply for division)

Example: 4 × -8 = -32

Example: -5 × -7 = 35
